Shift Ciphers

The simplest form of encryption. Moves letters by a fixed number of positions in the alphabet.
Security: None. Trivial to break via brute force (only 25 possible keys) or letter frequency analysis.
Usage: Puzzles, games, or obscuring text from casual glances (ROT13).

Polyalphabetic Ciphers

Uses multiple cipher alphabets to substitute plaintext, flattening the letter frequency distribution.
Security: Low to Medium. Much stronger than simple substitution, but vulnerable to statistical methods (Kasiski, IoC) if the message is significantly longer than the key.
Usage: Historically used for military/diplomatic comms (Vigenère, Enigma).

Dictionary & Substitution Ciphers

Replaces single characters or pairs (digraphs) based on keywords, grids, or symbols.
Security: Low. Vulnerable to frequency analysis. Digraph ciphers (Playfair) require more ciphertext (~50+ chars) to break than simple substitution.
Usage: Field encryption where equipment was limited (Playfair, ADFGVX).

Columnar & Transposition Ciphers

Rearranges the order of characters without changing their identity. Letter frequencies (monograms) remain identical to the plaintext.
Security: Low. Vulnerable to anagramming, bigram analysis, and multiple anagramming if messages share length/keys.
Usage: Often combined with substitution (Fractionated Morse, ADFGVX) to add diffusion.
